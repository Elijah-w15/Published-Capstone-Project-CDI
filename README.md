# Nanoporous Carbon for Water Desalination

For my senior college project, I worked with a team of engineers to develop a green method for generating a nanoporous carbon substrate used in continuous capacitive deionization, a way of desalinating water using only a carbon source and electricity. The work was published in the MDPI scientific journal *Molecules*.

**Read the paper:** https://www.mdpi.com/1420-3049/30/13/2848

## Process overview

The goal was to make a high surface area carbon out of a renewable source and use it to pull salts out of water with nothing but electricity.

1. **Start with algae:** We used algae as a renewable carbon source.
2. **Heat with water:** The algae was heated to 250 C with water in a sealed hydrothermal reactor, carbonizing the algae while the water pulled out polar impurities.
3. **Heat again under nitrogen to carbonize:** The resulting tar was then heated to 800 C under nitrogen, ensuring the carbon didn't combust.
4. **Activate with KOH:** The carbon was mixed with potassium hydroxide and heated again to 1000 C under nitrogen. This produced a huge network of tiny pores that gave the carbon high surface area in order to better hold the salts.
5. **Coat it onto electrodes:** The activated carbon was made into an organic slurry, coated onto graphite sheets, and then allowed to evaporate in order to form the coated electrodes.
6. **Run the cell:** The electrodes were assembled with ion-exchange membranes, and water with salts was run through the middle channel. A low voltage was applied, and the electricity pulled the ions across the membrane, and into the pockets of the activated carbon electrodes. By flipping the voltage, the electrodes could be cleaned for reuse.

The result was an eco-friendly, low-energy electrode material that performed on par with or better than comparable desalination systems in recent research.
